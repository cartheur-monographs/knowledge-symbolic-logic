# Berkeley as a Precursor to Embodied Machine Intelligence

## Working title

Berkeley as a Precursor to Embodied Machine Intelligence: Symbolic Logic, Control, and Behavior Before Adaptive Robotics

## Abstract

Edmund C. Berkeley is usually remembered as a writer who helped connect symbolic logic to computing machinery. That description is correct, but incomplete. This paper argues that Berkeley's `1959` *Symbolic Logic and Intelligent Machines* should also be read as an early contribution to embodied machine intelligence. Berkeley does not merely treat intelligence as formal symbol manipulation. He repeatedly defines intelligent machines in operational terms, ties logical form to hardware realization, and describes intelligent behavior as the coordinated interaction of inputs, outputs, memory, calculation, and control. Most importantly, his treatment of robots, machine activities, and the `algebra of states and events` moves beyond static logical form toward temporally extended, environment-coupled behavior. Berkeley does not yet reach the later adaptive and reinforcement-oriented framework found in David L. Heiserman's `1981` evolutionary machine intelligence, but he does mark an important intermediate position between algebraic logic and creature-like machine behavior. Read in this way, Berkeley appears not only as a symbolic logician of machines, but as a bridge figure in the history of embodied machine intelligence.

## Core claim

The central claim of this paper is that Berkeley should be interpreted as a bridge figure between symbolic logic and embodied machine behavior. He is not a full theorist of adaptive embodied AI in the later Heiserman sense, but neither is he adequately understood as a purely formal or disembodied logician. His work gives a logically rigorous account of how physically organized machines can sense, store, decide, and act over time.

## Research question

How should Berkeley's *Symbolic Logic and Intelligent Machines* be situated in the history of machine intelligence: as a work of symbolic logic applied to machinery, or as an early account of embodied, behavior-organizing intelligence?

## Thesis

Berkeley's work is best read as both, but with stronger embodied implications than standard histories usually acknowledge. He inherits formal machinery from Boole, Venn, and Shannon, yet uses that inheritance to theorize intelligent systems as physically realized, temporally organized, and behaviorally active machines. His account stops short of adaptive creature intelligence, but it clearly anticipates later embodied and control-oriented approaches.

## 1. The standard reading of Berkeley

Berkeley is commonly placed in the history of symbolic logic, early computing, and machine thought. That placement is justified by the explicit structure of *Symbolic Logic and Intelligent Machines*. The book clearly presents symbolic logic as a rigorous language for handling classes, statements, relations, and conditions, and it repeatedly emphasizes Boolean algebra as a useful tool for the design of computing and switching systems. Read at this level, Berkeley appears as a mediator between formal logic and computer engineering.

Yet this standard reading is too narrow. It captures the logical side of Berkeley's project, but it does not fully capture the way he defines intelligence, the kinds of machines he gathers under that heading, or the significance of his chapters on states, events, and robots. Berkeley is interested not merely in formal calculability. He is interested in the realization of reason-like competence in physical apparatus.

This wider reading becomes more plausible when Berkeley is placed in lineage. Boole provides the originating algebra of logic. Venn sharpens class logic, simplification, and symbolic treatment. Shannon demonstrates that Boolean relations can be realized in relay and switching circuits. Berkeley inherits that entire trajectory, but he uses it to ask what kinds of machines can perceive, store, control, and behave.

## 2. Logic embodied in machinery

One of Berkeley's most important moves is to insist that symbolic logic is not merely a way to describe reasoning in abstraction. It is also a way to design machinery. This is the decisive shift from logic as formal analysis to logic as implemented intelligence.

That shift depends on the historical bridge supplied by Shannon. Once Boolean algebra can be mapped onto relay and switching circuits, conditions and inferences are no longer confined to paper operations or mental acts. They can be built into hardware. Berkeley fully embraces this possibility. In his hands, classes, statements, and logical conditions become design elements for circuits, controls, and automatic systems.

This is already enough to distinguish Berkeley from a narrow intellectualist account of logic. The point is not only that the machine can calculate. It is that the machine can be organized so that its internal states and responses are governed by logical structure. Logic becomes operational.

The significance of this move is historical as well as conceptual. If Boole made it possible to imagine an algebra of thought, and Shannon made it possible to embody logical operations in circuits, Berkeley helps make it possible to think of intelligent behavior as something physically buildable. This does not yet amount to modern embodied AI, but it is a necessary step toward it.

## 3. Intelligent machines as organized systems

Berkeley's explicit definition of intelligent machines is one of the strongest pieces of evidence for an embodied reading. He does not define intelligence as theorem proving, language use, or abstract symbol manipulation taken alone. Instead he emphasizes capacities such as taking in information, retaining knowledge, responding appropriately to new situations, and succeeding in task performance.

This is an operational conception of intelligence. It shifts the focus from inner formal correctness alone to competent outward behavior. Berkeley reinforces this point by the range of machines he classifies as intelligent. He includes traffic light controllers, factory control systems, air-traffic equipment, game-playing machines, reading and recognizing devices, robots, and large digital computers. These are not all disembodied information processors. Many of them are directly engaged with environments, signals, motion, and control.

Berkeley also gives a five-part architecture for intelligent machines:

- input
- output
- storage or memory
- calculating capacity
- control

This architecture matters. It frames intelligence as the coordinated function of sensing, acting, remembering, processing, and directing. In later language, it resembles a minimal agent architecture. It is not yet adaptive in the way later behavioral systems become adaptive, but it is already a systems conception rather than a purely formal one.

Especially important is Berkeley's claim that the control unit may itself be subject to instructions calculated by the machine, allowing the machine in some sense to guide itself. This is a modest but real step away from purely fixed automation and toward autonomous organization.

## 4. States, events, and temporally extended behavior

If Berkeley had stopped with Boolean algebra and machine architecture, the case for an embodied reading would remain suggestive but incomplete. The strongest evidence comes later, in his treatment of the `algebra of states and events`.

A major limitation of purely static logic is that it does not, by itself, explain behavior over time. Agents do not merely hold true or false relations. They persist in conditions, undergo interruptions, react to signals, wait for triggers, and coordinate sequences of action. Berkeley directly addresses this problem by distinguishing states from events and by treating machine behavior as structured transitions among them.

This move is important because it adds temporality to machine intelligence. A state lasts for a time. An event is brief. Machine organization therefore requires more than static truth-functional combination; it requires a formal account of persistence, transition, delay, and sequencing. Berkeley's framework provides exactly that.

The conceptual result is significant. Intelligent behavior becomes something that can be described in terms of:

- ongoing conditions
- triggering occurrences
- transitions between modes
- durations and delays
- controlled sequences of operation

This is deeply compatible with later control-oriented and embodied approaches. Berkeley is not describing an abstract proof system here. He is describing how a machine can inhabit and move through structured patterns of activity.

## 5. Robots and behavioral programs

Berkeley's discussion of robots strengthens the embodied interpretation further. He defines a robot as a machine able to behave by itself, taking in sensations through sensing devices, producing actions through acting devices, and correlating sensation and action through circuits or mechanisms expressing instructions. This definition is much closer to an agent-based understanding of intelligence than to a purely symbolic one.

His examples are revealing. `Squee`, the robot squirrel, is described in terms of activities such as hunting, homing, and depositing. The machine is not simply computing. It is operating through a sequence of activities coordinated by conditions and program relays. Likewise, Berkeley's `robot animal` problem organizes the machine around behaviors such as searching for food, eating, sleeping, and escaping danger. Each behavior persists until a relevant event or condition causes a transition to another behavior.

What matters here is not merely that Berkeley mentions robots. It is that he treats behavior as mode organization. The machine has a repertoire of activities, a set of triggers, and rules for transition. That is already a behavioral architecture. The robot is not only a calculator with sensors attached. It is a system organized around condition-governed activity.

This is precisely why Berkeley can be read as a precursor to embodied machine intelligence. He understands that intelligence in machines is inseparable from organized behavior in time and in relation to signals from an environment.

## 6. Berkeley compared with Heiserman

The comparison with David Heiserman helps define both Berkeley's importance and his limits. In *Robot Intelligence ... with Experiments* (`1981`), Heiserman presents a layered account of evolutionary machine intelligence in which creatures move from random but constrained activity to memory-based adaptation and eventually to confidence-weighted generalization. In the EAMI framing, intelligence is lived by a creature in an environment. It senses, acts, remembers, adapts, and in later stages exhibits something close to anticipatory or homeostatic behavior.

Berkeley does not go this far. His machines are largely engineered top-down rather than adaptively self-modifying. He does not offer reinforcement learning in Heiserman's explicit sense, nor does he provide Beta-style response memory or Gamma-style confidence-driven generalization. His robot animal does not learn its environment through accumulated successful responses. Its modes and transitions are designed in advance.

And yet the gap between the two should not obscure their continuity. Berkeley already supplies several of the architectural and conceptual themes that later make Heiserman's creature intelligence intelligible:

- intelligence as physically realized organization
- behavior as transitions among activities
- sensing and acting as core machine capacities
- coupling to an environment
- internal control as the basis of outward competence

The contrast therefore should be framed carefully. Berkeley is not Heiserman avant la lettre. He is better understood as a precursor who establishes the control-and-behavior side of machine intelligence without yet arriving at adaptive creature intelligence. Heiserman extends the behavioral story by adding memory, reinforcement, confidence, and generalization.

## 7. Conclusion

Berkeley should not be reduced to a symbolic logician who happened to mention machines. His work deserves to be read as an effort to show how intelligence can be built into physically organized systems that sense, remember, control, and act over time. The historical materials in his book, especially the treatment of machine architecture, robots, and states and events, support a reading of Berkeley as an early contributor to embodied machine intelligence in a control-oriented sense.

This conclusion should be stated with care. Berkeley does not yet formulate adaptive, reinforcement-based, or homeostatic machine intelligence in the manner later found in Heiserman. But that limitation clarifies rather than weakens his importance. Berkeley occupies a crucial intermediate position. He links algebraic logic and logical circuitry to organized machine behavior. He shows how formal logical structure can govern temporally extended, physically embodied activity. In that sense he is not the endpoint of embodied machine intelligence, but he is part of its prehistory.
