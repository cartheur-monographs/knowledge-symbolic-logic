# Annotated Bibliography

This bibliography supports the paper draft `berkeley-embodied-machine-intelligence-draft.md`. It is organized in five bands:

1. primary sources
2. immediate contextual sources
3. cybernetics, control, and embodied-machine background
4. robotics and behavior-based comparison
5. secondary historical and philosophical interpretation

The most important discipline for the paper is that the thesis should remain primary-source driven. The later and broader materials are for framing, comparison, and careful expansion, not for replacing Berkeley with a retrospective story.

## Must-have

These are the references that directly carry the paper's central argument.

### Berkeley, Edmund C. *Symbolic Logic and Intelligent Machines*. New York: Reinhold Publishing Corporation, 1959.

This is the central primary source. It supplies the definition of intelligent machines, the five-part machine architecture of input, output, storage, calculating capacity, and control, the discussion of robots, and the crucial treatment of the `algebra of states and events`. The paper's main thesis depends on close reading of this book.

Role in paper:

- primary evidence
- source of key definitions
- source of the embodied-control interpretation

### Boole, George. *The Mathematical Analysis of Logic*. Cambridge, 1847.

This is one of Berkeley's acknowledged intellectual starting points. It helps establish the algebraic treatment of logic that later becomes available for machine realization.

Role in paper:

- historical lineage
- founding algebraic logic

### Boole, George. *An Investigation of the Laws of Thought*. London, 1854.

This is the Boole book Berkeley explicitly recalls discovering as decisive. It is important not only as background history but as a text Berkeley himself identified as formative.

Role in paper:

- direct Berkeley lineage
- conceptual origin of an algebra of thought

### Venn, John. *Symbolic Logic*. London: Macmillan, 1894.

This is the strongest Venn source for the paper. Berkeley explicitly cites Venn, borrows a Venn problem, and works in a class-logic and simplification tradition closely aligned with Venn's treatment.

Role in paper:

- direct secondary influence on Berkeley
- class logic and simplification

### Shannon, Claude E. "A Symbolic Analysis of Relay and Switching Circuits." *Transactions of the American Institute of Electrical Engineers* 57 (1938): 713-723.

This is the decisive bridge between logical algebra and hardware realization. For this paper, Shannon is what lets the argument move from symbolic logic to embodied control systems.

Role in paper:

- logic-to-circuit bridge
- historical mechanism for embodiment

### Heiserman, David L. *Robot Intelligence ... with Experiments*. Blue Ridge Summit, PA: TAB Books, 1981.

This is the most useful later comparison point. Heiserman gives the paper a way to distinguish Berkeley's control-and-behavior conception from later adaptive creature intelligence involving memory, confidence, and generalization.

Role in paper:

- controlled comparison
- later embodied and adaptive framework
- clarifies both Berkeley's importance and his limits

## Should-have

These references strengthen the paper's contextual and comparative framing.

### Basson, A. H., and D. J. O'Connor. *Introduction to Symbolic Logic*. Glencoe, IL: Free Press, 1960.

This is useful as a mainstream mid-century symbolic-logic reference point. It helps show what a more standard textbook presentation looked like around Berkeley's period and highlights how Berkeley is more machine-facing and behavior-oriented.

Role in paper:

- contextual contrast
- orthodox formal-logic baseline

### Wiener, Norbert. *Cybernetics: Or Control and Communication in the Animal and the Machine*. Cambridge, MA: MIT Press, 1948.

This is an important framing source if the paper wants to position Berkeley within the wider postwar conversation about control, communication, and machine organization. It is especially useful if the argument expands from symbolic logic to embodied machine intelligence in a broader historical field.

Role in paper:

- control and communication context
- shared postwar environment for machine intelligence discourse

### Ashby, W. Ross. *Design for a Brain*. London: Chapman and Hall, 1952.

Ashby is useful for bringing in adaptive organization, regulation, and machine behavior in a non-symbolic but still rigorous way. This can sharpen the paper's sense of what Berkeley does and does not provide.

Role in paper:

- adaptive systems comparison
- historical context for machine self-organization

### Ashby, W. Ross. *An Introduction to Cybernetics*. London: Chapman and Hall, 1956.

This is a cleaner systems-and-control reference than `Design for a Brain` in some respects. It can help frame states, regulation, feedback, and machine organization in language closer to the control side of Berkeley's work.

Role in paper:

- cybernetic systems framing
- vocabulary for control and organization

### Turing, Alan M. "Intelligent Machinery." 1948.

This is useful for broadening the period conversation around machine intelligence without losing historical discipline. It can help situate Berkeley among other mid-century attempts to conceptualize intelligent machines.

Role in paper:

- period comparison
- machine intelligence discourse

### Turing, Alan M. "Computing Machinery and Intelligence." *Mind* 59, no. 236 (1950): 433-460.

This is best used sparingly. It is a landmark text, but it can easily pull the paper toward the imitation-game tradition rather than Berkeley's control-and-behavior orientation. Still, it helps show what other machine-intelligence framings were available in the period.

Role in paper:

- contrastive period framing
- alternative model of machine intelligence

### Walter, W. Grey. *The Living Brain*. London: Duckworth, 1953.

Useful if the paper wants a stronger bridge to autonomous, sensorimotor, and creature-like machinery. Walter is especially valuable if the final version emphasizes embodied machine behavior rather than only logical architecture.

Role in paper:

- embodied autonomous machinery
- biological and robotic comparison

## Optional comparative

These sources are best used carefully and mostly for conceptual comparison rather than historical genealogy.

### Braitenberg, Valentino. *Vehicles: Experiments in Synthetic Psychology*. Cambridge, MA: MIT Press, 1984.

Braitenberg is excellent for later reflection on how simple sensorimotor couplings can generate apparently intelligent behavior. This can help readers understand why Berkeley's robot discussions matter, but it should be kept explicitly comparative because it is much later.

Role in paper:

- conceptual comparison
- later embodied intelligence perspective

### Brooks, Rodney A. "A Robust Layered Control System for a Mobile Robot." *IEEE Journal of Robotics and Automation* 2, no. 1 (1986): 14-23.

Brooks is helpful if the paper wants to point to later behavior-based robotics as a distant but relevant descendant of machine intelligence understood as active control and situated behavior. Again, this is comparative, not genealogical evidence of influence.

Role in paper:

- later behavior-based robotics comparison
- shows modern relevance of Berkeley's control emphasis

### Brooks, Rodney A. "Intelligence Without Representation." *Artificial Intelligence* 47, no. 1-3 (1991): 139-159.

This can help sharpen the contrast with Berkeley. Berkeley still works through logical form and structured conditions, whereas Brooks becomes more aggressively anti-representational. The comparison can therefore illuminate how unusual Berkeley's embodied logicalism really is.

Role in paper:

- conceptual contrast
- clarifies that Berkeley is embodied without abandoning logical structure

## Optional historical interpretation

These are not central, but they may help the paper read more like a piece of scholarship if needed.

### Secondary histories of symbolic logic

Use one or two compact, reliable histories to contextualize Boole and Venn without spending too much space on background exposition.

Role in paper:

- limited orientation
- helps avoid overexplaining basic logic history from scratch

### Secondary histories of cybernetics and early AI

Use these only if the paper needs a broader framing section or a literature review paragraph on where Berkeley fits relative to cybernetics, AI, and robotics.

Role in paper:

- historiographic placement
- broader field context

## Suggested citation hierarchy

If space is tight, the paper should weight the references like this:

1. Berkeley
2. Boole, Venn, Shannon
3. Heiserman
4. Basson
5. Wiener, Ashby, Walter
6. Turing
7. Braitenberg and Brooks

This keeps the paper anchored in Berkeley while still giving it a serious comparative frame.

## Practical guidance for the write-up

- Use Berkeley, Shannon, and Heiserman in the core argument.
- Use Boole and Venn in the lineage section.
- Use Basson briefly as an orthodox comparison.
- Use Wiener, Ashby, and Walter only where the embodied or control-oriented claim needs broader support.
- Use Brooks and Braitenberg, if at all, only in the conclusion or discussion section as later conceptual resonance.
